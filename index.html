The Manager's Guide

A poster-style guide explaining what "The Manager's Guide" means to us — including expectations and ways to achieve them.

Information Poster

The Manager's Guide

A simple framework for leading projects with clarity, consistency, and accountability.

Expectations

  • Set direction with clear goals, priorities and definitions of "done".
  • Communicate early — share updates before problems grow.
  • Protect quality — ensure testing, accessibility and standards.
  • Support the team — remove blockers and distribute work fairly.
  • Own outcomes — take responsibility for delivery and learn from results.

How to achieve them

  • Weekly plan: choose 3–5 goals, assign owners, set due dates.
  • Daily check-ins: 5 minutes to track progress and blockers.
  • Definition of done: checklist for quality (responsive, accessible, working links).
  • Review rhythm: demo progress, collect feedback, improve quickly.
  • Post-mortem: after delivery, write what worked and what to improve.

What it means to us

To us, "The Manager's Guide" is not about control — it's about creating a system where the team can do their best work. A good manager keeps expectations visible, makes progress measurable, and builds confidence through consistent execution.

SWOT analysis — always-evolving technology

By Khushbuben Khakhriya

Strengths
  • Efficiency through automation and better tools.
  • Innovation that creates new careers and industries.
  • Access to education, services and global markets.
Weaknesses
  • Complexity makes systems harder to secure and maintain.
  • Skill gaps as tools change faster than training.
  • Dependence on platforms, updates and connectivity.
Opportunities
  • Smarter services in healthcare, finance and education.
  • Business growth via digital reach and data-driven decisions.
  • Personalization for better user experiences.
Threats
  • Cybersecurity risks: breaches, ransomware, identity theft.
  • Misinformation amplified by fast-moving platforms and AI.
  • Inequality if benefits concentrate in a few groups.

Chosen aspect: AI-powered tools in everyday life

By Jenish Gadhiya

In today's world, technology is deeply embedded in almost every aspect of our daily lives. From the moment we wake up and check our phones, to using apps for banking, navigation, communication, and entertainment — we are constantly interacting with digital systems. However, what many people fail to consider is that these technologies carry real and significant vulnerabilities.

Personally, I believe it is important to acknowledge and be aware of these vulnerabilities rather than simply ignore them. Every device we use — whether a smartphone, laptop, or smart home device — collects data, connects to networks, and can be exploited if not properly secured. Ignoring this reality is not only naive but potentially harmful to ourselves and those around us.

For example, most people use the same password across multiple platforms, leave Bluetooth on in public spaces, or click on suspicious links without thinking twice. These small, everyday habits can lead to serious consequences such as identity theft, financial loss, and unauthorised access to private information.

That said, I also understand why many people choose to ignore these risks. Technology is designed to be convenient, and constantly thinking about security can feel exhausting or overly technical for the average user. However, basic digital hygiene — using strong and unique passwords, enabling two-factor authentication, and keeping software regularly updated — can significantly reduce risk without requiring expert-level knowledge.

In my opinion, acknowledging technology's vulnerabilities is not about living in fear. It is about being a responsible and informed user in an increasingly digital world. Awareness is the first and most powerful step toward protection — and it begins with a simple decision to pay attention.

AI in Academia  

Legal or Illegal?

This section explains the Traffic Light Model for Artificial Intelligence in academia. The examples below demonstrate illegal (Red), questionable (Yellow), and legal (Green) uses of AI tools in educational environments.

Group Task — Khushbuben Khakhriya & Jenish Gadhiya
🔴

Illegal Use of AI

Red represents unethical and prohibited AI usage in academia. These actions break academic integrity rules and may lead to plagiarism, disciplinary action, or failing grades.

Example 1: Full AI-Generated Assignment

A student asks ChatGPT to create an entire research paper and submits it without editing, understanding, or mentioning AI usage. The assignment is presented as completely original work even though the student did not create the content independently.

Example 2: AI During Online Exams

During an online examination, a student secretly uses AI tools to answer questions in real time. This is considered cheating because the answers are not created independently by the student.

Example 3: Fake Sources and Citations

A student copies AI-generated references and academic citations without checking whether the sources actually exist. This spreads false information and damages the credibility of academic research.

Academic misconduct and plagiarism
🟡

Questionable Use of AI

Yellow represents AI usage that may be acceptable in some situations but still requires caution, transparency, and teacher approval.

Example 1: AI Rewriting Large Sections

A student writes the assignment independently but uses AI to rewrite paragraphs, improve sentence structure, and make the language sound more academic. The core ideas are original, but the final wording relies heavily on AI.

Example 2: AI-Assisted Brainstorming

A student asks AI to generate research questions, outlines, and possible arguments before beginning the assignment. While this can support creativity, excessive dependence may reduce independent thinking and originality.

Example 3: Translation and Language Support

Non-native English speakers may use AI tools for grammar correction, translation, and academic language improvement. This can be helpful, but some institutions may require students to clearly disclose how AI was used.

Requires caution and transparency
🟢

Legal Use of AI

Green represents ethical and responsible AI usage that supports learning without replacing the student's own academic work.

Example 1: Learning Difficult Concepts

A student uses AI to better understand programming, mathematics, or scientific theories by asking for explanations, examples, and step-by-step guidance. The final work is still completed independently.

Example 2: Grammar and Proofreading Help

AI tools are used to check grammar, punctuation, spelling, and sentence clarity before submission. The ideas, analysis, and content remain fully created by the student.

Example 3: Study and Revision Support

A student uses AI to generate summaries, flashcards, quiz questions, or revision notes while preparing for exams. The AI supports learning rather than replacing the student's own work.

Ethical and responsible AI usage
